Sevoflurane and propofol are modern anesthetics used in Bosnia and Herzegovina for the past two decades. Initially, they were used to put adults under general anesthesia based on the recommendations of their manufacturers. Propofol is an intravenous anesthetic that can also be used for sedation in intensive care units. It is metabolized in the liver and excreted through urine. Sevoflurane is a completely fluorinated methyl isopropyl ether. In a study comparing general anesthesia using propofol and standard pediatric anesthesia practices, no research was found on preoperative and postoperative laboratory findings for comparison. Both anesthetics were administered after inducing sleep and muscle relaxation, followed by intubation with an appropriate endotracheal tube. Postoperative pain was treated with various analgesics. After waking up, patients were transferred to the Pediatric Surgery department of the University Clinical Center Sarajevo for further treatment. Blood sugar, creatinine, urea, AST, and ALT levels were measured in patients who received sevoflurane. The TIVA (total intravenous anesthesia) technique using propofol and the inhalation technique using sevoflurane, isoflurane, and enflurane all have advantages. TIVA requires minimal preparation, is easy to handle and control, and is efficient. Sevoflurane and propofol are preferred over other recently used anesthetics due to their simplicity, ease of use, and efficiency. Studies have compared sevoflurane-remifentanil anesthesia with propofol-remifentanil, sevoflurane-induced anesthesia with propofol-induced anesthesia in small children, and the effects of sevoflurane on serum creatinine and blood urea nitrogen concentrations in adults.
